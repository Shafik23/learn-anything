# Dose, Testing, And Safety

This page is a learning aid, not a dosing protocol. Vitamin D3 decisions can depend on age, pregnancy, baseline 25(OH)D, calcium intake, kidney function, malabsorption, bariatric surgery, osteoporosis treatment, granulomatous disease, medications, and prior kidney stones.

## Intake Anchors

US intake recommendations are expressed as total vitamin D from food, fortified food, beverages, and supplements.

| Anchor | Adult value | Meaning |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| RDA through age 70 | 600 IU/day, or 15 mcg/day | Intake expected to meet needs for most healthy people in this age range |
| RDA over age 70 | 800 IU/day, or 20 mcg/day | Higher intake recommendation for older adults |
| Adult UL | 4,000 IU/day, or 100 mcg/day | Highest average daily intake unlikely to pose risk for almost all adults |

The UL is not a target. It is a risk boundary for chronic total intake.

## Testing Anchors

The usual blood test is serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D, written 25(OH)D.

| 25(OH)D value | Common interpretation from NASEM/NIH context |
| --- | --- |
| Less than 12 ng/mL, or less than 30 nmol/L | Risk of deficiency |
| 12 to 20 ng/mL, or 30 to 50 nmol/L | Some people may be at risk of inadequacy |
| Around 20 ng/mL, or 50 nmol/L | Meets the needs of most people by NASEM bone-health criteria |
| Above 50 to 60 ng/mL, or 125 to 150 nmol/L | Range NASEM advises avoiding |

Different organizations and clinicians may use different thresholds. A lab number should be interpreted with symptoms, calcium, kidney context, bone risk, diet, sun exposure, medications, and the reason the test was ordered.

## Routine Screening

For asymptomatic, community-dwelling, nonpregnant adults, the USPSTF concludes evidence is insufficient to say routine vitamin D screening has a favorable balance of benefits and harms. The Endocrine Society's 2024 guideline also suggests against routine 25(OH)D testing in generally healthy adults without established indications.

That does not mean testing is never useful. It means routine population-style testing is not the same as testing for a specific clinical reason.

## Supplementation Context

Broad patterns from current guidance:

- Generally healthy adults under 75 are usually pointed back to the RDA rather than extra empiric supplementation above the RDA.
- Adults over 75, pregnancy, and high-risk prediabetes are contexts where the Endocrine Society 2024 guideline suggests empiric supplementation for specific prevention questions.
- When vitamin D treatment is indicated in adults 50 and older, daily lower-dose vitamin D is preferred over non-daily high-dose vitamin D.
- Deficiency treatment is a medical context, not the same question as routine supplementation in people without known deficiency.

## Toxicity Pattern

Vitamin D toxicity is usually caused by excessive supplement intake, not ordinary sun exposure. The central problem is too much calcium activity.

Watch concepts:

- hypercalcemia: high blood calcium
- hypercalciuria: high urine calcium
- kidney stones
- dehydration, excessive thirst, frequent urination
- nausea, vomiting, weakness, confusion, pain, and loss of appetite
- soft-tissue calcification and kidney injury in severe cases

Higher risk contexts deserve clinician review before supplement changes:

- kidney disease or history of kidney stones
- high calcium, high parathyroid hormone, or unexplained calcium problems
- sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, lymphoma, or other granulomatous disease context
- malabsorption or bariatric surgery
- osteoporosis or antiresorptive/anabolic bone medications
- pregnancy
- digoxin, thiazide diuretics, steroids, antiseizure drugs, weight-loss drugs that reduce fat absorption, or complex medication lists

## Practical Reading Checklist

When you see a vitamin D3 claim, ask:

- Is it about deficiency treatment, normal adequacy, or disease prevention?
- Was baseline 25(OH)D measured?
- Was the outcome a lab marker, symptom, bone density, fracture, fall, infection, cancer, cardiovascular event, or mortality?
- Was the population generally healthy, older, pregnant, institutionalized, deficient, osteoporotic, malabsorbing, or medically complex?
- Was vitamin D given daily or as intermittent high doses?
- Was calcium also given?
- Did the study report harms such as hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, stones, or falls?
