# Vitamin D3 Core Map

Use this page to orient the topic before getting lost in dosing arguments.

## The Pathway

```text
Inputs
  sunlight UVB
  foods and fortified foods
  supplements

Forms
  vitamin D3: cholecalciferol
  vitamin D2: ergocalciferol

Status marker
  liver converts vitamin D -> 25-hydroxyvitamin D, or 25(OH)D

Active hormone
  kidney and some tissues convert 25(OH)D -> 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D

Main classical effects
  calcium absorption
  phosphorus handling
  parathyroid hormone regulation
  bone mineralization
```

## The Three Questions

Vitamin D3 conversations become clearer when separated into three questions.

| Question | What it asks | Evidence style |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Adequacy | Am I meeting normal nutrient needs? | RDA, diet, sun exposure, status distributions |
| Deficiency | Is there a medical problem to diagnose and treat? | Symptoms, risk factors, labs, clinician judgment |
| Prevention | Does extra vitamin D prevent a disease in people without deficiency? | Randomized trials and guideline reviews |

Most confusion comes from using evidence for one question to answer another.

## Forms And Labels

- Vitamin D3 is cholecalciferol.
- Vitamin D2 is ergocalciferol.
- Supplement labels usually list vitamin D in IU, micrograms, or both.
- Conversion: 1 mcg vitamin D = 40 IU. So 25 mcg = 1,000 IU.
- "High potency" is a marketing phrase, not proof that a dose is appropriate.

## Lab Terms

| Term | Meaning | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 25(OH)D | 25-hydroxyvitamin D | Main blood marker for vitamin D status |
| 1,25(OH)2D | 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D | Active hormone; not the usual status test |
| ng/mL | Common US lab unit | 20 ng/mL = about 50 nmol/L |
| nmol/L | Common international unit | Multiply ng/mL by 2.5 |
| Calcium | Blood mineral affected by vitamin D | High calcium is central to toxicity risk |

## Evidence Levels

Strong anchors:

- Severe deficiency harms bone mineralization.
- Vitamin D is needed for normal calcium and phosphorus handling.
- Excessive supplementation can cause hypercalcemia and serious toxicity.

Middle ground:

- Some people have higher risk of low status.
- Supplementation can be appropriate in defined medical contexts.
- Some outcomes may differ by baseline status, age, pregnancy, institutionalization, calcium intake, and trial design.

Weak or commonly overstated:

- Low vitamin D status proves vitamin D caused the disease.
- Raising 25(OH)D above ordinary adequacy improves broad health outcomes.
- Megadoses are safer or better because vitamin D is "natural."
